# Research: The Launch Genre ("Toss" Games)

*Deep-research report, 2026-07-18. The founder's beloved "paper penguin airplane" maps to two games, both covered: **Learn to Fly** (Light Bringer Games, Kongregate 2009) — a penguin sliding down a ramp learning to fly; and **Flight** (Krin Juangbhanich, Armor Games 2010) — a paper airplane carrying a girl's letter around the world. The memory fusion is itself telling: the genre is so uniform in feel that individual games blur together. Grounds the progression-dial guidance in `02-generation-pipeline.md` and the progression-archetype rubric items in `03-quality-rubric.md`.*

## 1. Game-by-game anatomy

**Yeti Sports: Pingu Throw (2004)** — genre ur-ancestor. One click: a yeti swats a falling penguin; score = slide distance. No upgrades, no progression — pure timing + score-sharing. ~300M players across the series; described as "the most successful online game series ever" via pure viral spread. Proved one-input + distance-number = phenomenon.

**Nanaca Crash (2005)** — Japanese refinement. Angle+power launch of a boy hit by a bicycle; he ricochets off pedestrians whose collision effects are semi-random (some boost, some kill momentum, rare "special" combos). Limited mid-air "Aerial Crash" clicks add sparse agency. Explicitly slot-machine-like: records require lucky character sequences, so players re-roll endlessly. Its host site crashed from traffic within days of launch.

**Kitten Cannon (2005, Dan Fleming)** — per Newgrounds founder Tom Fulp, this "started the Western craze of games that involved tossing things into the air and seeing how far they can bounce." Angle + oscillating power bar; then pure spectacle: explosives and trampolines extend flight, spikes/fly traps end it. No economy — the variance itself (bomb chains vs. instant spike death) was the hook. Shallow angles (10–20°) beat high arcs, a discoverable "skill" layer.

**Hedgehog Launch (Armor Games, 2008)** — added the *goal-capped campaign*: reach space in as few days as possible. Slingshot launch, mid-air steering via jetpack, coins and bounce platforms feed money; money buys launcher height, rocket efficiency, parachutes, radar. Payout formula = altitude + airtime + coins, so *every* run pays. The "days taken" counter creates a speedrun meta.

**Learn to Fly (2009) + LTF2 (2011) + LTF3 (2016)** — the genre's most polished arc. A penguin, insulted by "Kiwipedia" calling penguins flightless, learns to fly. Ramp slide → angle control with arrows → glider → rockets with fuel management. Six upgrade tracks (ramp height, acceleration, air resistance, glider, rockets, fuel); money awarded per run for height, distance, and airtime (multiplier up to 3x). Deliberately removed random obstacles — pure deterministic skill+upgrades, strategy shifts as gear changes (early water-bouncing becomes counterproductive once you have a glider). Ends by showing completion time in days with "Think you can do better?" — a direct replay taunt. **LTF2's story is a revenge arc**: the penguin, humiliated by a "Flying Penguin… more like cannonball!" viral video after crashing into an iceberg, breaks out of the hospital and builds launch rigs to *destroy the iceberg*. LTF3: an insulting email ("you can fly but not to space") sends him building a moon rocket. The comedy premise (flightless-bird grudge) carries the whole franchise.

**Flight (2010)** — the emotional outlier. A girl named Sandy throws a paper airplane letter to Santa asking to see her mother; the plane travels London→Paris→Egypt→Kenya→Japan as strangers pick it up and re-throw it, each reinterpreting the letter. Mouse-drag throw; stars = currency; upgrades: throw strength, aerodynamics, rudder (unlocks arrow-key steering), boost. Reviewers called it "breezily enjoyable… charm and polish," criticized the empty late-game sky and bittersweet-but-unsatisfying ending. Proof the genre can carry *sentiment*, not just comedy.

**Toss the Turtle (Newgrounds, 2009)** — gore-comedy pole. Cannon-launch a turtle, then *shoot him mid-air* with purchasable guns (machine guns → golden gun → nukes) to keep him flying; bounce off explosive/bouncy hazards. Cartoon gore as reward spectacle. 6M+ views, 4.4/5, Daily Feature. The mid-air shooting is a clever agency patch: player always has a button to press.

**Burrito Bison (Juicy Beast, 2011) + Revenge + Launcha Libre (2016)** — genre's apex refinement. A luchador slingshotted across Candy Land, body-slamming gummy bears that burst (deliberate design choice: gummies = gore-without-blood, all-ages "violence"). Elastic-pull launch timing, rocket-slam recharge from squashing gummies, doors broken only at speed (visible progress gates). Formal-elements analysis highlights: money per squash, upgrades to cable (launch power) and lotion (speed retention on bounce), and — crucially — "there is no loser in the end of the game; victory is assured through persistence." Notably it *began as a runner* that never felt right and was redesigned into a launcher; the launcher loop is what worked.

## 2. Why one click is enough: the addiction machinery

- **Launch → watch → earn → upgrade → launch.** Input is front-loaded (one aimed throw), then the game plays itself while you spectate. Cognitive load ≈ zero; anticipation ≈ high.
- **No wasted run.** Every attempt converts to currency; a "bad" run still buys the next upgrade. This inverts the failure economics of skill games — failure *is* progress banking. Game-design literature frames this as the core retention rule: the player must never feel they went backward.
- **The upgrade always in view.** Shops dangle the next affordable item; runs are 30–90 seconds, so "one more upgrade" is always 1–3 runs away. Short run + adjacent reward = hour-long sessions.
- **Slot-machine variance.** Bounces, explosive chains, gummy/character collisions create high-variance outcomes on identical inputs — near-miss psychology (the run that *almost* broke your record) is the re-roll trigger. Nanaca Crash and Kitten Cannon are nearly pure variance; Learn to Fly is nearly pure determinism; the sweet spot (Burrito Bison) blends both.
- **Two dueling scoreboards.** Personal-best distance (infinite chase) + campaign goal with a "days taken" grade (finite, replayable). Learn to Fly's ending literally taunts you with your day count.
- **Numbers going up on three axes at once** (per-run distance, cumulative money, upgrade tiers) so *something* improves every single run.

## 3. Physics as the fun engine

Ragdoll/projectile spectacle is entertaining with zero mid-flight input because physics generates *authored-feeling* drama for free: each bounce is a mini cliffhanger with legible cause and effect. The genre's agency trick is **sparse high-leverage inputs**: one launch decision, plus optional mid-air taps (rocket fuel, aerial crashes, shooting the turtle, rudder steering) — enough to feel responsible for good runs while blaming physics for bad ones. Critically, **upgrades are visible on the body**: new glider strapped to the penguin, bigger cannon, longer elastic — purchases change what you *see*, not just hidden stats, and change optimal strategy (glider makes water-bounces bad), so the game keeps re-teaching itself.

## 4. Shareability

Distance is a single, universally legible brag number — "how far did you get?" needs no genre literacy, which fueled office/schoolyard score wars (Yeti Sports' 300M players came almost entirely from viral pass-along). Absurd one-line premises (yeti swats penguin; penguin declares war on iceberg; luchador vs. gummy bears; turtle + nuke) are meme-able before the game is even played. Learn to Fly 2 even *diegetically* references viral humiliation videos.

## Generator-applicable principles

1. **Bank every attempt.** Any generated game for casual players should convert failure into a persistent resource; no run may earn zero. Progression is the safety net that replaces skill.
2. **One high-leverage input.** Design around a single aimed/timed action, then let simulation carry the run; add at most one optional mid-run button (boost/shoot/steer) for felt agency.
3. **Physics is free content.** A bouncy, chain-reaction world generates spectacle and variance without authored levels — the cheapest fun per line of generated code.
4. **Determinism + variance blend.** Player-controlled launch (skill attribution for wins) + environmental randomness (luck attribution for losses) = maximal re-roll motivation.
5. **Runs of 30–90s; next reward 1–3 runs away.** Tune upgrade prices so the shop always shows something almost affordable.
6. **Upgrades must be visible and strategy-changing**, not stat-only — new gear on the sprite, new physics behavior.
7. **Three simultaneous number-go-up axes**: per-run score, cumulative currency, permanent upgrade tiers.
8. **Dual goals**: infinite personal-best chase + finite completable campaign graded on efficiency ("done in X days — think you can do better?").
9. **Guaranteed victory through persistence** (Burrito Bison's "no loser" structure): the game ends in triumph for everyone; only *speed* differentiates players.
10. **A one-sentence absurd premise** (X launches Y to do Z) does the work of narrative, marketing, and meme-ability at once; petty-revenge comedy (LTF) and sincere sentiment (Flight) both work.
11. **Family-safe "gore"**: exploding gummies/cartoon ragdolls give visceral impact feedback without blood — impact spectacle is the reward channel.
12. **One legible brag metric** (distance) makes scores socially portable — build share/compare around a single number.
